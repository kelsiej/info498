Religion in Tweets

An analysis of the Twitter accounts of the Pope and the Dalai Lama for the final project of INFO 498 F: Religion and Information Technology

References:


Introduction


Throughout this site, I will be exploring and analyzing the twitter accounts of the pope, the leader of the Catholic Church, and the Dalai Lama, the leader of Tibetan Buddhism as religious digital artefacts.


Religion is one of the most important social constructs, with only 14% of people living outside of a faith. It can be defined well by Catherine Albanese as “a system of symbols (creed, code, cultus) by means of which people (a community) orient themselves in the world with reference to both ordinary and extraordinary powers, meanings, and values.” Without understanding how religions function, it is also impossible to fully understand society, making them an important topic to study.

I will be looking at these two religions, Christianity and Tibetan Buddhism, through the lens of their leaders Twitter accounts. Twitter is a popular social media platform that allows its users to post “tweets,” which can be up to 140 characters long. Users can follow others on the platform, as well as comment on, repost, and like their tweets. Twitter and other forms of social media have brought a whole new level of communication for a community, allowing leaders to share directly with their followers with not much intermediary, a feat that would not have been possible in the past.

I plan to analyze the perspectives of the Pope and the Dalai Lama and report on any similarities and differences that I find focusing on the following themes; stories and ideas, symbols and images, values and practices, and stereotypes and norms. My educational objective is to learn about how these themes manifest in the Pope's and the Dalai Lama's tweets, I will look at the frequency of related terms in their tweets and what response they get from other users. The significance and uniqueness of my research comes from my analysis of what aspects of their religion the pope and the Dalai Lama choose to share with society, and the following levels of interaction and responses to what is shared. My results will be relevant to anyone interested in how elements of religion manifest in popular social media platforms used by society today.
